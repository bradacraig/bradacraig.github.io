Identity and Values

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

Recently my band Mini Simmons was in the midst of organising our final show. We were selling tickets online via a service provided by a local venue and receiving daily updates on how we were tracking. Upon reviewing the breakdown of the costs we realised that the venue had accidentally been charging each purchaser an extra 10% due to GST being added twice. As far as the venue was concerned, they were still getting their cut so we technically could have just pocketed the money and carried on living. Ticket purchasers were also unaware as most would have just assumed the total price was a little more expensive this time around. However, as a band we decided that it was not right to keep the extra money and the ethical thing to do would be to email the ticket holders, inform them of the error and refund the extra charge.

As we were weighing up the decision, we thought about integrity and how important it is to be honest and open in what we do to keep our band's reputation solid and our fans' trust intact. We wanted to be fair, so we owned up to the mistake and decided to give back the extra money, making sure our fans weren't left out of pocket. Taking responsibility was key for us; we admitted the error and made things right, showing that we're serious about being answerable for our actions and sticking to high ethical standards.

Reflecting on this decision I am pleased that we decided to refund the extra charge. Personally, I would have felt guilty keeping money that we had not earned honestly. I like to think that I have a good sense of judgement when it comes to situations like this and after weighing up the options I believe we made the right choice.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

Family is an integral part of my life and I’m lucky to have inherited strong, positive values from them that make me who I am today. I grew up with my mum and a younger brother, my dad passed away when I was young so we lived with my grandparents until I moved away for Uni. I like to think of my family as embodying a work hard, play hard mentality. Physical activity and fitness have always played a prominent role in our lives and we’re lucky enough to still be able to share activities like surfing together. We are a social bunch and often enjoy getting together with our extended family for drinks, dinners and sometimes the odd family sing-a-long. My partner Emily and I have been together for over three years now and I’m forever grateful for her positivity and zest for life. Her encouragement and support have given me the confidence to make the move back to study this year. We’re also lucky that we share a lot of the same interests playing music and surfing together as well as a passion for cooking and travel.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

  • Strong work ethic and determination: I began learning the basics of coding and web development around one year ago while still working full-time. As my time was limited I got into the habit of waking up at 5 am and using the hour and a half before I had to leave for work to get some learning and practice in. It’s a practice and habit I’ve come to enjoy and has helped me to implement better time management throughout my life. I aim to stick to this schedule throughout my time at Dev Academy and use the time to practice auxiliary skills like typing (which I’m a little slow at) and to work on personal projects like redesigning a website for my friend’s landscaping business.
  • Curiosity and enthusiasm to learn: I’m thankful that I genuinely enjoy the learning process and look forward to developing new skills. I’m hoping that this will keep me motivated throughout the course and drive my learning journey.
  • Open-mindedness and flexibility: I’m aware of what I know and maybe even more aware of what I don’t know. Now, more than ever I’m grateful for my ability to keep an open mind and have my views challenged or changed. There’s always more than one way to solve a problem and just because I come up with an initial solution that feels right for me, it doesn’t mean that it’s the best or most appropriate for the situation. I’m willing to accept that I might be wrong and happy to try someone else’s idea or solution in place of my own. I think compromise is a key part of working effectively in a team.

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

  • Fear of failure and mistakes: Mistakes are going to be made somewhere along this learning journey, and I’m not always going to get it right the first time. However, I need to be mindful of this and accept that this is all part of the process, rather than letting the fear stop me from making progress. I think the idea of breaking problems down into smaller chunks and being methodical rather than trying to speed through tasks should help me here.
  • Perfectionism: I often have an idealised version of the thing I’m trying to create. This is great in some respects as it allows me to aim high and try to be the best I can possibly be. On the flip side, however, it can also slow me down massively as I try to strive for absolute perfection when sometimes it isn’t necessary. Striking a balance between perfectionism and efficiency will be something I focus on throughout my learning journey.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

Recently my band was busy rehearsing for our last show as our singer and bass player are moving overseas. We've been playing together for around seven years now and have all become very close friends. With our final show looming, I was adamant that we should make the most of our last rehearsals and work really hard. Unfortunately life often gets in the way. Not everyone was able to commit to the same level of intensity and rehearsal schedule as I'd hoped for. Often one member would have to work later than expected and couldn't make it. While this was frustrating, I managed it by making the best of who was available at the time. In some ways, not having everyone present made you focus on different aspects of the song and performance that you might not have seen or heard had everyone been there. I found it beneficial to focus on these silver linings rather than get too disappointed at the lack of availability and commitment. This taught me to manage expectations and was a good lesson in seeing things from someone else's perspective.

In addition to this, because we are all such good friends the rehearsals can often drift into social catch-up occasions. While initially I was slightly annoyed at the lack of focus, I realised that sometimes it's better to be a bit more lenient and not to rock the boat too much in an effort to preserve the morale and enthusiasm of the group. Instead, I would slowly steer the conversation back to the task at hand and act as the leader rather than letting it descend into a disorganised mess.

If I were to approach this situation again I could make my intentions clearer from the outset as to what I expected from the sessions and implement more structure by allocating focus time countered by more frequent breaks for socialising, compromise being key here.
